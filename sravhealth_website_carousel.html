02 / 08
Before anything else

She arrives asking
three silent questions.

She is not reading your website carefully. She is scanning it for answers to three implicit questions. If your homepage answers all three above the fold, she stays. If not, she leaves.

1
"Is this person for someone like me?"
Your headline answers this — or it doesn't. If it leads with your modality, you've lost her before she reads the second line. If it names her symptom or her situation, she leans forward.
2
"Has this worked for people like me?"
A client quote, a specific outcome, a Google review. She needs to see herself in someone who has already been where she is — and come out the other side. This is the job of social proof.
3
"What do I do next?"
One clear next step. Not three options. Not a newsletter sign-up competing with a booking link competing with a free guide. One CTA that removes the friction between interest and action.
Answer all three in the first scroll. She should not have to look for any of them.
Slide 3 / 8 — The headline
Srav Health
03 / 08
Must-have #1

The headline.
Your single most
important element.

If your headline does not make the right person feel immediately understood, nothing else on the page matters. She has already left.

✕
Fails — leads with modality
"Ayurvedic practitioner specialising in holistic wellness and balanced living"
She has no idea if this is for her. "Holistic wellness" could be anyone. She has seen this sentence a hundred times.
✕
Fails — credential-first
"NAMA-certified Ayurvedic Practitioner serving the Bay Area and online"
She does not know what NAMA means. Credentials build trust — but only after she already feels understood. Not before.
✓
Works — names her situation
"Finally understand why you're exhausted, wired, and hormonally stuck — and build a protocol that actually sticks"
She recognises herself in the first four words. She knows this is for her. She reads the second line.
✓
Works — answers the failed-by-system feeling
"Your labs are normal. You still feel terrible. There's a reason — and an Ayurvedic answer for it."
Mirrors the exact sentence in her head. Immediately credible because it names the gap conventional medicine left.
The headline is not about you. It is about the moment she is in when she finds you.
Slide 4 / 8 — Social Proof
Srav Health
04 / 08
Must-have #2

Social proof.
Above the fold.
Always.

She is asking "has this worked for someone like me?" before she has even scrolled. Answer that question before she has to look for it.

✓ Social proof that converts
Specific outcomes — "Her cycles regulated in 12 weeks." Not "improved wellness."
Google reviews visible on site — she trusts a third-party platform more than your own quotes
Client stories — one paragraph that describes where someone was before and what changed. In her words.
Number of clients served or years in practice — simple credibility signals that require no explanation
✕ Social proof that doesn't land
"She really helped me!" — vague, no specifics, no outcome visible
Only your credentials listed — expertise without outcomes doesn't convert
Testimonials buried at the bottom of the page — she never scrolls that far on a first visit
Generic praise without a name or context — feels fabricated even when it isn't
If you have 5 Google reviews, they belong on your homepage. Not just on Google.

Slide 6 / 8 — What kills conversions
Srav Health
06 / 08
The conversion killers

Six things quietly
costing you bookings.

Each one is fixable in under an hour. Most practitioners have three or more of these live on their site right now.

01
Headline leads with your credential
She does not know what AHC or NAMACB means. Start with her problem, not your qualification.
02
No photo or video above the fold
She is making a health decision. She needs to see you before she trusts you. A professional photo builds more trust than a paragraph of credentials.
03
Booking page requires too many steps
Every click between "I want to book" and "I am booked" is a drop-off point. One click to Calendly is the standard. Three clicks and a form is not.
04
No social proof visible without scrolling
Most visitors on mobile never scroll past the first screen. If your reviews are at the bottom, they are invisible to the majority of your traffic.
05
Vague outcome language
"Balance," "harmony," "wellness" — these words mean nothing specific. "Her energy returned in 6 weeks" means something. Be specific or be ignored.
06
Multiple CTAs competing for attention
When everything is a priority, nothing is. Pick one action you want her to take. Put everything else second.
Fix these six. Before you spend a dollar on advertising, fix these six.
Slide 7 / 8 — The booking page
Srav Health
07 / 08
The highest-leverage page on your site

Your booking page
deserves more
attention than it gets.

Most practitioners spend months on their homepage and 20 minutes on their booking page. That is backwards. This is where interest becomes a client.

Must be on the page
What the page must answer
What happens on the call — exactly. Remove the unknown.
How long — 20 minutes. Free. No obligation. Say it plainly.
Two or three client quotes — specific outcomes, not general praise
One FAQ answering the biggest objection she has — usually cost or time
After the call
Send a summary within 2 hours
Recap what she shared in her words
Your specific recommendation for her — not a generic offer
One link to book and pay. Nothing else competing for attention.
Remove friction everywhere
Make it effortless to say yes
Calendly embedded directly — no external link click
Automated reminder 24 hours before — reduces no-shows by 30–40%
If she visits but doesn't book — one automated follow-up email 48 hours later
Payment link sent after call — not before. Commitment first, payment second.
The booking page rule
By the time she lands here, the decision is mostly made.
Your job is not to convince her on this page. It is to remove the last reason not to click.
