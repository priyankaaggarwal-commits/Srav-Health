Positioning

Selecting Your Positioning
as an Integrative Practitioner

Srav Health · February 2026 · 11 min read

Positioning is the single highest-leverage decision you'll make for your practice. Not your website design, not your Instagram strategy, not your pricing. Your positioning. Because every piece of marketing you ever create flows downstream from itand if the positioning is wrong, everything downstream underperforms.

Yet it's the thing most practitioners get wrongnot from lack of intelligence, but because the way practitioners are trained to describe their work is almost perfectly calibrated to not convert clients.

The problem with how practitioners describe themselves

In clinical settings, precision of language matters. You speak in the vocabulary of your traditiondosha, biomarkers, pattern discrimination, meridian pathways. This is appropriate when communicating with colleagues, writing case notes, or attending a conference.

It is not appropriate when writing your website homepage or Instagram bio. Because your potential client doesn't speak that languageand more importantly, she's not starting from curiosity about your methodology. She's starting from a symptom. A frustration. An exhaustion with being dismissed.

Here's what weak positioning sounds likeand why each version fails:

Genericfails to differentiate

"I help women achieve balance through Ayurvedic medicine."

Specificconnects immediately

"I help women in their 30s and 40s get to the root cause of hormonal fatigue and irregular cyclesusing an Ayurvedic protocol designed for modern life."

Credential-ledclient-irrelevant

"BAMS-certified Ayurvedic practitioner specializing in Tridosha assessment and Panchakarma detoxification."

Outcome-ledanswers "what's in it for me?"

"Medically trained in Ayurvedic medicine, I help women understand why they feel exhausted and unwelland build a protocol that actually sticks."

Tradition-firstmisses the bridge

"Root-cause healing through the wisdom of Ayurveda, honoring the connection between mind, body, and spirit."

Evidence-bridgedearns trust on arrival

"Combining 5,000 years of Ayurvedic tradition with current research on the gut-hormone axis, I help women find answers conventional medicine hasn't given them."

The three dimensions of strong positioning

Strong positioning for an integrative practitioner sits at the intersection of three things: who you serve most effectively, what they're struggling with most urgently, and what makes your approach distinctly credible.

Dimension 1 Your ideal client is more specific than you think. "Women with hormonal issues" is not an ideal client profile. "Women 35–50 in SF who've had their thyroid and hormones checked by three different doctors, been told everything is normal, and are still completely exhausted" is. The more specifically you can describe her, the more precisely you can speak to her and the more she feels you already understand her before she's even booked a call.

Dimension 2 The problem you solve has to be felt, not diagnosed. Your potential client doesn't yet know her root cause that's why she's coming to you. She knows what she feels: fatigue, brain fog, mood crashes, irregular periods, gut issues that won't resolve. Your positioning should name those feelings, not the clinical terminology behind them.

Dimension 3 Your credibility bridge must be earned, not assumed. The mistake practitioners make is assuming that "Ayurvedic practitioner" or "licensed acupuncturist" is self-evidently credible to a new potential client. It isn'tnot yet. You need to bridge the gap between your credentials and her trust. That bridge is built through outcomes, evidence, and empathy.

The positioning question isn't "what do I do?" It's "what does she feel before she finds me, what does she feel after working with me, and what about my approach makes that transformation credible?" Answer those three questions and you have your positioning.

The niche trap and how to avoid it

Every practitioner fears that niching down means turning away clients. In practice, the opposite is true. The more specifically you speak to a particular type of woman with a particular kind of problem, the more powerfully she feels you understand herand the more likely she is to book, commit, and refer others exactly like her.

You are not turning away women who don't match your niche. You are becoming the obvious choice for women who do. Your calendar fills faster with fewer, better-fit clientsand your outcomes improve because you're working with people for whom your methodology is most effective.

How to test your positioning before you commit

Before rewriting your entire website, test your new positioning in the lowest-stakes way possible: your Instagram bio or LinkedIn summary. Update it to reflect your sharpened positioning and watch for three things over the next 30 days: Do the inquiries you receive describe the exact problem you named? Do referrals come in who are better-fit than before? Do current clients say "yes, that's exactly it" when they read your bio?

If yes on all three, you've found your positioning. Now build everything else from there.

One practical exercise

Pull up the last ten client intake forms or initial consultation notes. What words do your clients use to describe how they felt before they came to you? Copy those exact phrases. That languageunedited, in their wordsis almost certainly stronger positioning material than anything you'd write from scratch.

The best positioning is usually discovered, not invented.
